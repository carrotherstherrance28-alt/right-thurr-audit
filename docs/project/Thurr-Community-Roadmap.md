# Thurr Community Roadmap

This roadmap defines how the Thurr brand can expand into community after consistent content, proof, and support capacity exist.

Community is a later trust layer, not the first offer.

## Position

Thurr should stay service-led before it becomes community-led.

The first job is to prove that Thurr Solutions can see business leaks, build practical automation systems, explain the work clearly, and help owners make better operational decisions.

Community becomes useful when people are already paying attention to the build and asking for a place to go deeper.

## Timing Rule

Do not launch a public community until these signals exist:

- 8-12 weeks of consistent LinkedIn-first publishing.
- A repeatable weekly content rhythm that does not depend on last-minute scrambling.
- At least 3-5 client-safe case studies, buildout examples, internal Right Thurr proof assets, or workflow teardown posts.
- A clear intake path for people who want implementation help.
- A simple support and moderation process.
- A specific reason for people to gather beyond generic AI automation learning.

The community should follow demand. It should not be used to create demand from scratch.

## Primary Audience

Start with business owners and operators who care about:

- Missed leads
- Slow follow-up
- Manual quoting and intake
- Poor visibility into daily activity
- Disconnected tools
- Better sales process handoffs
- Practical AI automation without learning every tool themselves

Secondary audience:

- Builders, assistants, and small teams who help owners run intake, follow-up, reporting, and client communication.

Avoid making the first community a broad AI education room. Thurr Solutions should stay close to implementation, business infrastructure, and owner outcomes.

## Community Promise

Possible operating promise:

```txt
A practical room for owners who want better intake, follow-up, reporting, and automation systems.
```

Shorter version:

```txt
Build the system behind the business.
```

Use `We build. You profit.` as a brand promise around implementation, not as a guaranteed community outcome.

## Roadmap Phases

### Phase 0: Proof Before Community

Goal: earn attention before asking people to join anything.

Build:

- LinkedIn-first content rhythm.
- Behind-the-scenes build posts.
- Workflow teardown posts.
- Local service business automation examples.
- Founder/operator lessons.
- Soft CTAs for buildout plans, audits, and workflow reviews.

Success signals:

- People comment with real business problems.
- People ask for examples, diagrams, templates, or audits.
- Buildout plan requests come from content.
- The same questions appear often enough to justify a shared space.

### Phase 1: Private Interest List

Goal: collect demand without creating a support burden.

Build:

- Simple waitlist or interest form.
- One clear question: what system do you want fixed first?
- Tags for niche, pain point, business size, and urgency.
- Manual follow-up path for strong-fit owners.

Possible form questions:

```txt
What kind of business do you run?
Where do leads or client requests currently get stuck?
What would you want help building first?
Do you want implementation help, examples, or peer discussion?
```

Success signals:

- 25-50 qualified people join the interest list.
- Repeated themes appear across responses.
- At least a few people are willing to pay for implementation, audits, or a small working session.

### Phase 2: Small Private Room

Goal: test usefulness with a manageable group.

Build:

- Private invite-only group for owners, early clients, and warm leads.
- Weekly practical prompt or teardown.
- Monthly live buildout review or workflow clinic.
- Lightweight resource library.
- Clear rules around privacy and client/customer data.

Possible channels:

- Announcements
- Workflow Questions
- Intake And Follow-Up
- Buildout Reviews
- Wins And Fixes
- Resources

Operating rules:

- No customer private data in public posts.
- No spam pitches.
- Keep advice practical.
- Owners can ask for teardown help, but deep implementation routes back to Thurr Solutions services.

Success signals:

- Members ask specific process questions.
- Weekly posts get replies without chasing.
- Live reviews create clear content and service insights.
- The group produces better sales conversations, not just more noise.

### Phase 3: Owner Automation Lab

Goal: turn community into a repeatable learning and implementation layer.

Build:

- Monthly theme such as lead follow-up, quoting, reporting, review requests, or handoffs.
- One simple system map per month.
- One walkthrough or teardown.
- One implementation checklist.
- Optional paid workshop or build sprint.

Possible monthly structure:

```txt
Week 1: Diagnose the leak.
Week 2: Map the workflow.
Week 3: Build or improve one automation.
Week 4: Review results and next steps.
```

Success signals:

- Members return for multiple themes.
- Paid workshops or audits convert naturally.
- Community questions become content ideas.
- Service delivery improves because patterns are easier to spot.

### Phase 4: Right Thurr User Group

Goal: support product users only after the app has real onboarding and report workflows.

Build:

- User feedback room.
- Product education posts.
- Blueprint examples.
- Feature request intake.
- Known issue updates.
- Office-hours style support.

Do not launch this phase until Right Thurr has real users, owner authentication, report workflows, and a clear support boundary.

## Content-To-Community Loop

Use public content to test what deserves a community layer.

Loop:

1. Publish a practical post.
2. Track comments, DMs, saves, and buildout requests.
3. Turn repeated questions into a teardown, checklist, or live review.
4. Route implementation-ready owners to Thurr Solutions.
5. Save common patterns for future community programming.

Best source material:

- AI Business Buildout Plan sections
- Autopilot Blueprint examples
- n8n intake workflows
- Supabase persistence path
- Discord alert flow
- Thurnos draft generation
- Local service business workflow maps
- Client-safe before/after process stories

## Platform Order

Recommended order:

1. LinkedIn content and comments.
2. Simple email list or waitlist.
3. Private Discord, Slack, or Circle-style room if demand is real.
4. YouTube or video walkthroughs after there is a repeatable recording rhythm.
5. Paid workshops, templates, or build sprints after proof exists.

Do not add multiple platforms just to look bigger. Every new platform creates another place to maintain trust.

## Offer Boundaries

Community can support:

- Workflow diagnosis
- Practical education
- Buildout reviews
- Accountability around implementation
- Examples and teardown discussion
- Product feedback later

Community should not replace:

- Thurr Solutions implementation services
- Private client consulting
- Done-for-you automation builds
- Client-specific strategy
- Support for tools Thurr does not own or manage

## Brand Boundary

Owned Thurr community assets can use the Thurr brand system:

- Orange, ink, paper, bold borders
- R+T monogram when appropriate
- Directional arrow language
- Confident builder tone
- `We build. You profit.`
- `Ideas do not pay you. Systems do.`

Client diagnostic communities or client-owned groups should be re-skinned and should not inherit Right Thurr or Thurr Solutions visual branding by default.

## What Not To Build Yet

Hold these until demand is proven:

- Large public Skool-style education community.
- Full course funnel.
- Certification program.
- Multi-tier membership.
- Template marketplace.
- Daily live support.
- Broad AI prompts library detached from business implementation.

These can become valuable later, but launching them too early would pull attention away from the core service engine.

## First Viable Community Experiment

When ready, start with this:

```txt
Name: Thurr Owner Automation Lab
Format: Private invite-only room
Audience: owners and operators with real intake, follow-up, or reporting leaks
Cadence: one weekly prompt, one monthly live workflow review
CTA: request a buildout plan or bring one process for teardown
Duration: 30-day test
Success measure: useful questions, qualified service conversations, and repeat engagement
```

Keep the first experiment small enough to run manually.

The goal is not to prove that Thurr can host a room. The goal is to prove that the room makes owners clearer, builds trust faster, and points the right people toward systems that should exist.
